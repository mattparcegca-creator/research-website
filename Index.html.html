CHAPTER ONE

Introduction

In the current digital age, students' everyday lives now revolve on technology. According to Rideout, Peebles, Mann, and Robb (2022), teens use digital devices for an average of 8 hours and 39 minutes per day. The rising use of electronic devices and the Internet has been linked to an increase in cyberbullying. Although students receive instruction on how to utilize technology effectively, they are not often given guidance on how to act morally and responsibly when using it online.

 

Since cyberbullying is one of the major problems that has risen because of technology's rapid progression, students tend to have more destructive behavior online. Even while school policies and awareness attempts about internet safety are becoming common, many adolescents may still not know enough to recognize, stop, or handle cyberbullying. While existing literature confirms that Grade 8 students are generally aware of the existence of cyberbullying, there remains a significant gap in understanding the extent and depth of this knowledge. Even while school policies and awareness attempts about internet safety are becoming increasingly common, numerous adolescents might still not know enough to recognize, stop, or handle instances of cyberbullying. Numerous studies have created anti-bullying programs and educational interventions to reduce bullying incidents because of the detrimental effects bullying has on a child's development and future on a social, physical, and psychological level (Ng et al., 2022). Knowing what Grade 8 pupils know is crucial because it can assist teachers, parents, and school officials in creating preventative initiatives and guidance plans that are more successful. Students who are well-informed are more likely to behave responsibly online and ask for assistance when needed. Furthermore, research indicates that focused awareness campaigns enhance teenagers' capacity to identify cyberbullying and create coping strategies, underscoring the importance of education in preventive and intervention initiatives (Yurdakul & Ayhan, 2022).

 

However, there is a research gap regarding the students' functional knowledge—specifically, their understanding of legal consequences, technical reporting tools, and the role of social media algorithms. This study seeks to determine if Grade 8 students at Great Christian Academy possess the practical knowledge required to effectively handle and report cyberbullying incidents, rather than just identifying them.

With the help of social networks, teenagers can effortlessly keep in contact with their loved ones and easily express themselves through different forms of socialization, but the privilege of expressing oneself can come with negativity as well. Cyberbullying escalated because bullying moved from in-person to digital. With the events of the COVID-19 pandemic, victims who were cyberbullied became more prevalent from 17% in 2019 to 23% in 2021 (Patchin & Hinduja, 2023).

Cyberbullying happens with every teen worldwide. As stated by the research conducted by Cook et al. With the results of the surveys, 11% of bullying happened via text messages, and 7.9% in video games. Additionally, the percentage of respondents who have encountered non-social media harassment was 6.8%, while the portion of the reported email bullying was 3.3%. Based on the research of UNICEF 2024, as cyberbullying progresses, the victims continually feel mentally distressed, self-conscious, and frightened. The victims undergo humiliation, disengagement with their hobbies and interests, and physical issues such as insomnia and headaches due to the pressure. With the extreme effects of cyberbullying, it unfortunately leads to suicide. (Chopra, 2022). With the persistent nature of cyberbullying, it can happen anywhere and at any time. It makes victims’ suffering worse, even when they are alone at home and not in class (Santos, 2024). As stated by the research of Patchin et al. 2. Knowledge and Awareness of Students on Cyberbullying

Due to the quick development of social media sites like Instagram and TikTok as well as online communication, students are being exposed to more types of cyberbullying. As a result, their degree of awareness and expertise is crucial for identifying risky online activities, comprehending digital legalities and responsibilities, and putting methods in place for reducing risks.

2.1 Recognition of Digital Rights and Cyber-Legislation

The integration of technology into the lives of students has become essential in the modern digital era. While these technological advancements offer significant benefits for learning and communication, they also present various challenges. Consequently, the global prevalence of cybercrime has increased, creating potential threats to individual safety and digital security (Toso et al., 2023).

The literature suggests that the recognition of digital rights and cyber-legislation serves as a critical deterrent against online misconduct. Alhadidi et al. (2024) argue that increasing students’ literacy regarding cybercrime and its corresponding legal frameworks functions as a proactive protective mechanism; this knowledge not only discourages individuals from engaging in illegal online activities but also reduces their susceptibility to victimization. Crucially, this recognition empowers students to transition from passive bystanders to active participants in digital justice, as those aware of their digital rights are more inclined to report offenses. However, the depth of this legal recognition appears to be contingent upon academic background. Titi (2025) observes a significant disparity in awareness levels, noting that students in technical disciplines—such as cybersecurity and computer science—possess a more robust understanding of these legal protections than their non-technical counterparts. Collectively, these studies indicate that academic exposure to technology-related curricula is a primary determinant in how effectively students recognize their rights and apply cyber-legislation to prevent digital threats, including cyberbullying. These findings highlight how a lack of literacy in cybersecurity and digital rights significantly increases student vulnerability to cyber threats, online harassment, and the broader spectrum of cyberbullying. Therefore, these results underscore the necessity of assessing current awareness levels to develop more effective educational interventions.

2.2 Ethical and Social Obligations in Digital Environments

According to the research by Arató et al., (2022), social and personal protective factors have a significant impact on participation in cyberbullying, especially cybervictimization. Strong self-regulation abilities, supportive peer interactions, and parental guidance are essential for preventing students’ from engaging in cyberbullying. These elements emphasize the significance of intimate social contexts in influencing students' digital experiences by enhancing coping mechanisms, enhancing emotional resilience, and lowering susceptibility to negative online interactions.

While protective factors operate at the individual level, students’ online experiences are fundamentally dictated by the ethical and social obligations of broader technological and regulatory systems. Recent research suggests that digital environments are not neutral spaces but are active structures shaped by corporate and legal infrastructure. Kantha et al. (2024) highlight critical ethical concerns regarding AI-driven systems and algorithmic profiling, noting that without robust technical knowledge and transparency, digital rights such as privacy and autonomy remain highly vulnerable. Furthermore, Guenduez et al. (2025) demonstrate that global digital ethics frameworks remain fragmented, leading to an uneven prioritization of security over profit-driven innovation. These structural gaps represent a failure of systemic obligation, creating "lawless" digital spaces where harms like cyberbullying flourish. By failing to provide standardized protections, these platforms effectively shift the heavy burden of risk management onto the knowledge and resilience of individuals and families, rather than addressing safety at the policy level.

Furthermore, Li et al. (2023) suggest that cognitive knowledge—specifically the ability to identify digital red flags and understand the permanence of online actions—is just as vital as emotional resilience. Without a clear foundational knowledge of platform safety tools and reporting protocols, students remain vulnerable even in regulated environments. students exhibit lower awareness and report only 6% weekly rates, indicating that their limited understanding of cyberbullying may reduce both recognition and reporting. In contrast, high school students show moderate weekly rates (25%), suggesting that growing experience and digital literacy may contribute to better recognition and coping strategies, even though gaps in effective prevention remain.

Together, these findings suggest that preventing cyberbullying and protecting students' mental health requires both strong digital regulations and supportive welfare and educational interventions.

2.3 Technical Knowledge in Online Risk Reduction

As modern society becomes more tech-dependent, securing digital data from sophisticated cyber threats has emerged as a multi-faceted and complex challenge (Chauhan et al., 2025). According to the Pew Research Center (2022), 46% of U.S. teens have experienced at least one form of cyberbullying, with 31% of those victims identifying their physical appearance as the primary reason for being targeted. While these figures reflect a growing digital concern, they also underscore a critical knowledge gap concerning the practical skills needed to maintain privacy and reduce risk in digital spaces.

Data from the Pew Research Center (2023) indicates that YouTube, TikTok, Snapchat, and Instagram remain the dominant online platforms among teens, highlighting the specific digital landscapes where technical risk reduction knowledge is most urgently required. These platforms serve as a primary conduit for information, significantly influencing how students acquire the necessary abilities in digital environments (Zakir et al., 2025). According to Santelices (2025), the implementation of targeted educational interventions significantly strengthens students’ proficiency in identifying and technically mitigating digital threats. Collectively, these studies corroborate the idea that social media significantly shapes students' technical proficiency and their broader understanding of cybersecurity and cyberbullying.

Altogether, research indicates that despite being 'constant users' of social media, students frequently encounter a significant deficiency in consistent, technical education regarding the effective use of platform safety tools (Hendry et al., 2023). For this reason, students have become heavily reliant on social platforms, resulting in a significant disparity in their technical proficiency depending on the quality and accuracy of their chosen information sources.

3. Influence of Age in cyberbullying Knowledge

Age is another indicator of cyberbullying involvement due to age variations in face-to-face bullying involvement. According to the Wright and Wachs, (2023), elementary school students are more prone than middle and high school students to engage in physical acts of hostility. Due to growing language skills and the significance of peer connection, verbal and relational forms become increasingly prominent in middle school, raising awareness of cyberbullying in early adolescence. Cyberbullying, which is frequently perceived as indirect bullying, increases during adolescence before decreasing in late adolescence and early adulthood, influencing age-specific knowledge patterns.

Cyberbullying engagement, whether as a perpetrator, victim, or witness, is substantially associated with depression across student groups, building on age-driven involvement trends. Victims report higher levels of rage, fear, and despair, as well as lower levels of self-esteem, school satisfaction, and overall happiness than their peers who are not participating. School performance declines over time for people who engage in externalizing activities like aggression and internalizing problems like worry. (Song et al., 2024). However, because there are no immediate victim reactions and little observer reinforcement, cyberbullying gives bullies smaller rewards, which may account for the uneven age trends in awareness and involvement. The necessity for status-aware therapies is highlighted by the gradual loss in academic performance among those who engage in externalizing behaviors like aggressiveness and internalizing issues like worry. (Suraseth and Koraneekij, 2024).

According to the findings of the Cyberbullying Research Center, from 33.6% in 2016 to 58.2% in 2025, the percentage of children who report having experienced cyberbullying in their lives has increased substantially. Middle school students (ages 12 to 14) show the highest exposure, reflecting the period of intense social interaction and digital engagement. Recent 30-day rates also rose sharply, from 16.5% to 32.7%, and disproportionately affected early adolescents — a group with emerging but still limited risk awareness of online victimization. Younger primary students exhibit lower awareness and report only 6% weekly rates, indicating that their limited understanding of cyberbullying may reduce both recognition and reporting. In contrast, high school students show moderate weekly rates (25%), suggesting that growing experience and digital literacy may contribute to better recognition and coping strategies, even though gaps in effective prevention remain.

These age patterns demonstrate that knowledge and awareness of cyberbullying change as children mature: younger students with lesser understanding are less likely to recognize harmful online behaviors, early adolescents experience high exposure with insufficient knowledge to protect themselves, and older adolescents show improved, though still imperfect, awareness. Therefore, to align cyberbullying knowledge with developmental exposure risks, age-tailored education and prevention strategies are essential.

4. Sex-Based Disparities in Cyberbullying Knowledge

In the contemporary digital landscape, significant sex-based disparities have emerged regarding cyberbullying awareness, necessitating a comprehensive assessment of how men and women understand and respond to online harassment. Studies indicate that while the overall framework of digital awareness is comparable across sexes, subtle differences exist in how specific digital risks are prioritized and interconnected (Yurayat, 2025). This suggests that even when general knowledge levels appear equal, the specific ways in which individuals navigate cyber-threats may vary. This divergence in mitigation strategies is further explored by Kakakhel (2023), whose findings indicate that female participants generally possess lower levels of knowledge and awareness regarding cyber-crimes compared to their male counterparts.

The sex-based knowledge gap reflects varying degrees of technical expertise and vulnerability with regard to cyberbullying and more general cybersecurity protocols. In a study by Marinoni et al. (2023), it was found that female participants were more frequently exposed to cyber-victimization, a trend largely attributed to deficiencies in their knowledge regarding cyberbullying. This suggests that the disparity in awareness is not merely an academic concern but a direct factor in increasing victimization risks among specific student demographics. Recent data from the Cyberbullying Research Center (2025) reveals a significant shift in gender dynamics regarding online harassment. While previous studies typically indicated that female students were more prone to victimization, the 2025 findings suggest that male students have become more frequently targeted, representing a notable departure from historical patterns.

Altogether, these findings suggest that there is a significant disparity between the sexes regarding cyberbullying knowledge, which acts as a critical factor in their overall vulnerability to online threats. This implies that while both men and women are equally susceptible to becoming victims, they experience differing levels of risk based on their specific knowledge gaps. Consequently, while the threat is universal, the nature of their vulnerability is distinct to their gender.

methodology

 

During the Academic Year 2025–2026, Great Christian Academy Grade 8 pupils' understanding of cyberbullying was evaluated using a qualitative analysis research methodology. To provide an objective representation of the population, a total of 20 respondents were chosen using simple random selection. A questionnaire created by the researcher, especially to assess the students' awareness, knowledge, and individual viewpoints on cyberbullying, was used to collect data.

 

Before conducting the research, the data collection process began with the process of randomly selecting 20 Grade 8 students. The students were provided with sufficient time to respond to the open-ended questions given to them. The researchers used a Likert scale analysis to systematically organize the collected data. In order to ascertain the students' overall level of awareness, the weighted mean of the quantitative data from the scales was calculated and given a verbal interpretation. All replies were kept completely anonymous, with no personal identifiers being recorded, in order to preserve the process's integrity. The data collected was utilized only for this academic experiment.

Data gathering procedure

To accomplish the study's goals, Grade 8 students participated in an in-person survey. This kind of process was used so that the researchers could give prompt explanations about the questionnaire. Because the assignment was given to them immediately, the students completed it with ease and skill.

The researchers stayed in the classroom to lead the activity and keep the atmosphere calm and concentrated during the data gathering process. In order to prevent the Grade 8 children from feeling rushed, a generous amount of time was allocated, enabling them to thoughtfully consider each question. The goal of this strategy was to reduce participant pressure and promote more deliberate, precise answers.

To guarantee the accuracy of the results, the researchers carried out a systematic data procedure once the questions were addressed. To ensure the complete integrity of this study, every form was carefully reviewed, and any incomplete or unintelligible data were removed from the final count. Finally, we employed numerical coding in our legitimate responses before encoding them into a digital spreadsheet for analysis in order to protect the students' anonymous identities.

The researchers used a variety of statistical techniques, including mean, median, mode, standard deviation, and kurtosis. Following these computations, tables and graphs will be used to show the total data in a clear, organized manner for ease of comprehension.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Statistical Tool and Treatment of Data

 

The data collected about the Grade 8 Students’ knowledge of cyberbullying will be objectively analyzed and interpreted using the following statistical tools:

 

The researchers will employ several statistical tools to analyze the gathered data comprehensively. The Arithmetic Mean will be used to determine the level of cyberbullying knowledge among Grade 8 Students based on their calculated average, where a higher mean is indicative of a comprehensive understanding and a lower mean suggests a deficiency in knowledge. To provide a balanced view, the Median will be utilized to identify the exact midpoint of the scores among the Grade 8 Students, ensuring that the data remains resistant to outliers and is not distorted by students with exceptionally high or low knowledge levels. Furthermore, the Mode will be used to identify the most frequent score obtained by the Grade 8 Students, allowing the researchers to determine the predominant level of knowledge and whether the majority of the group is knowledgeable. To measure consistency, the Standard Deviation will be applied to identify whether the knowledge levels of Grade 8 Students are mostly the same or widely varied, revealing any significant gaps between those who are informed and those who are not. Finally, the distribution of the data will be analyzed through Skewness, which measures the symmetry of the Grade 8 Students’ score distribution to see if the population leans toward the higher or lower end, and Kurtosis, which determines if the Grade 8 Students’ levels of knowledge are heavily clustered around the average or spread out evenly across different levels of understanding
